Acid-Base Chapter Notes

1. Definitions and Theories of Acids and Bases

Arrhenius Theory

  • Acid: Produces H+ ions in aqueous solution.
    Example: HCl → H+ + Cl–
  • Base: Produces OH– ions in aqueous solution.
    Example: NaOH → Na+ + OH–

Bronsted-Lowry Theory

  • Acid: Proton donor.
  • Base: Proton acceptor.
    Example: NH3 + H2O → NH4+ + OH–

Lewis Theory

  • Acid: Electron pair acceptor.
  • Base: Electron pair donor.
    Example: BF3 + NH3 → BF3:NH3

2. pH and pOH Calculations

  • pH: Measure of acidity, pH = –log[H+]
  • pOH: Measure of basicity, pOH = –log[OH–]
  • Relation: pH + pOH = 14 (at 25°C)

Example: If [H+] = 1 × 10–3, then:

  • pH = –log(1 × 10–3) = 3
  • pOH = 14 – 3 = 11

3. Strength of Acids and Bases

  • Strong Acids: Completely ionize in water.
    Examples: HCl, HNO3, H2SO4
  • Weak Acids: Partially ionize in water.
    Example: CH3COOH
  • Strong Bases: Completely dissociate in water.
    Examples: NaOH, KOH
  • Weak Bases: Partially dissociate in water.
    Example: NH3

4. Salt Hydrolysis

  • Determines whether a salt solution is acidic, basic, or neutral.
  • Key Concept: Hydrolysis depends on the nature of the parent acid and base.

Examples:

  • Strong acid + Strong base: Neutral salt (e.g., NaCl).
  • Strong acid + Weak base: Acidic salt (e.g., NH4Cl).
  • Weak acid + Strong base: Basic salt (e.g., CH3COONa).
  • Weak acid + Weak base: Depends on relative strength.

5. Ka and Kb (Acid and Base Dissociation Constants)

  • Ka: Acid dissociation constant.
    Larger Ka = stronger acid.
    Example: For CH3COOH, Ka = 1.8 × 10–5
  • Kb: Base dissociation constant.
    Larger Kb = stronger base.

Relation: Ka × Kb = Kw (ionic product of water = 1 × 10–14)

Difference between Ka and pKa:

  • pKa: –log(Ka)
  • Smaller pKa = stronger acid.
    Example: If Ka = 1.8 × 10–5, pKa = –log(1.8 × 10–5) ≈ 4.74

6. Types of Oxides

  • Acidic Oxides: React with water to form acids.
    Example: CO2, SO2
  • Basic Oxides: React with water to form bases.
    Example: Na2O, CaO
  • Amphoteric Oxides: React with both acids and bases.
    Example: Al2O3, ZnO
  • Neutral Oxides: Do not react with water, acids, or bases.
    Example: CO, N2O

7. Formulas and Examples for pKa and pKb Calculations

  • pKa + pKb = 14

Example:

  • For NH4+:
    • Ka = 5.6 × 10–10, pKa = –log(5.6 × 10–10) = 9.25
    • pKb = 14 – pKa = 14 – 9.25 = 4.75

8. Math for Exam Preparation

Buffer Solution

  • pH = pKa + log([Salt]/[Acid])
  • Example: For CH3COOH and CH3COONa with pKa = 4.76 and [Salt]/[Acid] = 10:
    pH = 4.76 + log(10) = 4.76 + 1 = 5.76

Henderson-Hasselbalch Equation for Bases

  • pOH = pKb + log([Salt]/[Base])

9. Key Points for Exam

  • Memorize strong acids and bases.
  • Practice pH and pOH calculations.
  • Know the relation: Ka × Kb = Kw.
  • Understand salt hydrolysis and buffer solutions.
  • Familiarize yourself with oxide types.
